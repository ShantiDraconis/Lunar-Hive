# Harappan Axis

## Overview

The **Harappan (Indus Valley) script** is an ancient writing system used by the Indus Valley Civilization (c. 3300–1300 BCE) in what is now Pakistan and northwestern India.

**Status**: Undeciphered.

The Lunar Hive approaches Harappan research with:
- **Rigor**: Every claim must be falsifiable
- **Transparency**: Speculation is labeled clearly
- **Evidence**: Hypotheses must be testable
- **Humility**: We acknowledge what we don't know

---

## Why This Matters

### Historical Significance
The Indus Valley Civilization was:
- One of the earliest urban cultures (contemporary with Mesopotamia and Egypt)
- Highly sophisticated (standardized weights, urban planning, drainage systems)
- Geographically vast (covering ~1 million km²)
- Largely mysterious (due to undeciphered script)

### Linguistic Mystery
- **~400 distinct symbols** identified
- **~4,000 inscriptions** known (mostly short)
- **No bilingual texts** (unlike Rosetta Stone for Egyptian)
- **No clear descendant language** established
- **Multiple competing theories** about language family

### Epistemological Case Study
Harappan decipherment is a perfect example of:
- Working with incomplete data
- Distinguishing hypothesis from fact
- Importance of falsifiability
- How speculation becomes knowledge (or doesn't)

## Current Theories

### 1. Dravidian Language Hypothesis
**Proponents**: Asko Parpola, others  
**Claim**: Harappan script represents an early Dravidian language  
**Evidence**:
- Proto-Dravidian presence in South Asia
- Some phonetic/morphological pattern matches
- Geographic continuity

**Status**: Hypothesis, not proven  
**Falsifiable by**: Finding contradictory linguistic patterns, bilingual texts showing different language

### 2. Indo-Aryan/Vedic Hypothesis
**Proponents**: Some nationalist scholars  
**Claim**: Script represents early Indo-Aryan/Sanskrit  
**Evidence**: Mostly circumstantial, cultural continuity claims

**Status**: Weak hypothesis, not widely accepted  
**Problems**: Chronology issues, linguistic evidence weak

### 3. Language Isolate
**Claim**: Harappan language has no known relatives  
**Evidence**: Inability to connect it to known families

**Status**: Null hypothesis (what we default to without evidence)

### 4. Not a Full Writing System
**Claim**: Symbols are logographic/ideographic, not representing full language  
**Evidence**: Short inscriptions, limited symbol use, no long texts

**Status**: Possible, debated  
**Counter-evidence**: Complexity and structure suggest linguistic encoding

## Methodological Challenges

### Data Limitations
- **Inscription length**: Average ~5 symbols (too short for statistical analysis)
- **Material**: Mostly seals and pottery (limited contexts)
- **Preservation**: 4,000+ year gap (no continuous tradition)
- **No Rosetta Stone**: No bilingual texts to anchor translation

### Analytical Difficulties
- **Symbol variants**: Are similar symbols the same or different?
- **Direction**: Right-to-left, left-to-right, or both?
- **Segmentation**: Where do words/morphemes begin and end?
- **Function**: Administrative? Religious? Commercial? All?

### Verification Problem
**How do we know if a decipherment is correct?**
- Internal consistency (does it follow patterns?)
- External validation (matches known history?)
- Predictive power (explains previously unexplained inscriptions?)
- Falsifiability (can it be tested and potentially proven wrong?)

**This is hard. Most proposed decipherments fail verification.**

## Falsifiability Standards

Every hypothesis about Harappan script must specify:

1. **What would prove it correct?**
   - Example: Finding bilingual text that confirms phonetic values

2. **What would prove it wrong?**
   - Example: Finding inscriptions that violate proposed grammatical rules

3. **What evidence currently supports it?**
   - Statistical patterns, linguistic parallels, etc.

4. **What evidence contradicts it?**
   - Counter-examples, failed predictions

**If a claim can't be falsified, it's not a scientific hypothesis.**
