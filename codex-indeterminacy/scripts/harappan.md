## Overview

**Time period:** ~3300-1900 BCE (Mature Harappan period)
**Geographic extent:** Indus Valley and surrounding regions (modern Pakistan, northwest India)
**Corpus size:** ~4000 inscriptions
**Decipherment status:** **Undeciphered** - no consensus on readings

## Basic Facts

### Script Characteristics

**Direction of writing:**
- Primarily right-to-left (consensus view)
- Some disputed cases of boustrophedon (alternating direction)
- Evidence: Symbol orientation, layout patterns

**Symbol count:**
- Estimates range from 400 to 700 distinct signs
- Core set of ~100-150 frequently used signs
- Many hapax legomena (single occurrences)
- Variation may reflect regional/temporal differences or scribal styles

**Text length:**
- Very short inscriptions (median: 4-5 symbols)
- Longest inscription: ~26 symbols

- Suggests limited text types or highly abbreviated writing

**Materials:**
- Seals (most common, typically stone or terracotta)
- Pottery (inscribed or painted)
- Metal tools and objects
- Clay tablets (rare)
- Ivory/bone (occasional)

## Corpus Analysis

### Seal Inscriptions

**Characteristics:**
- Square or rectangular seals
- Symbol text at top
- Animal or figure below (boss or depiction)
- Boss with hole on back (for handling)

**Common patterns:**
- 2-5 symbols per seal
- Particular symbol sequences repeat
- May represent names, titles, or ownership marks

**Function (hypothesized):**
- Trade identification
- Administrative purposes
- Property markers
- Personal or clan identity

**Confidence:** Medium (function inferred from context)

### Pottery Inscriptions

**Characteristics:**
- Often single symbol or very short sequence
- Scratched or painted
- May be potter's marks or content labels

**Pattern:**
- Different symbol distribution than seals
- Simpler inscriptions
- May be separate symbol subset

**Confidence:** Low-Medium (limited analysis)

### Miniature Tablets

**Rare examples:**
- Small clay tablets
- Longer texts (rare)
- May represent different text genre

**Significance:**
- Could provide more syntactic information
- But too few to draw strong conclusions

## Structural Hypotheses

### Text Structure

**Hypothesis 1: Name + Title**
- Initial symbols: Personal name
- Final symbols: Title or clan affiliation
- Middle symbols: Additional qualifiers

**Evidence:** Positional regularities, seal function
**Confidence:** Low-Medium

**Hypothesis 2: Commodity + Quantity**
- Symbols indicate traded goods
- Numbers indicate amounts
- Short texts suitable for labels

**Evidence:** Trade context, brief texts
**Confidence:** Low

**Hypothesis 3: Grammatical Structure**
- Initial: Subject/topic
- Medial: Verb/predicate
- Final: Object/grammatical marker

**Evidence:** Typological comparison, position patterns
**Confidence:** Low (very speculative)

### Writing System Type

**Hypothesis A: Logographic**
- Each symbol = word or morpheme
- Like Chinese characters
- Would explain large symbol count

**Problem:** 400-700 seems small for pure logographic
**Confidence:** Low

**Hypothesis B: Logographic-Syllabic Mix**
- Some symbols = words (logograms)
- Some symbols = syllables (phonograms)
- Like Sumerian, Egyptian, Maya
- Would explain symbol count and patterns

**Support:** Comparable symbol count to mixed systems
**Confidence:** Medium (common pattern in ancient scripts)

**Hypothesis C: Syllabary**
- Each symbol = syllable (CV, CVC, V)
- Like Linear B
- Symbol count fits syllabary range

**Support:** Right symbol count for syllabary
**Confidence:** Low-Medium

**Hypothesis D: Abjad/Abugida**
- Consonants written, vowels implied or marked
- Like Phoenician (abjad) or Brahmi (abugida)
- Would explain moderate symbol count

**Support:** Later Indic scripts are abugidas
**Confidence:** Low (but cannot rule out)

## Language Affiliation Hypotheses

### Dravidian Hypothesis

**Proponents:** Multiple scholars (Parpola, others)
**Evidence:**
- Geographic: Dravidian languages present in south India
- Some vocabulary items proposed (fish, star, etc.)
- Structural parallels suggested

**Challenges:**
- No definitive proof
- Counter-proposals exist
- Many proposed readings disputed

**Confidence:** Low (one of several competing hypotheses)

### Indo-Aryan Hypothesis

**Proponents:** Some scholars
**Evidence:**
- Later dominance of Indo-Aryan in region
- Possible Vedic connections

**Challenges:**
- Chronology problem (Harappan earlier than clear Vedic)
- Geographic issues
- Limited compelling evidence

**Confidence:** Very low (less favored currently)

### Language Isolate Hypothesis

**Proposal:** Harappan language unrelated to known families
**Evidence:**
- Cannot prove relationship to any family definitively
- May represent extinct language lineage

**Challenge:** Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence
**Confidence:** Unknown (null hypothesis)

### Multilingual Hypothesis

**Proposal:** Multiple languages written with one script
**Evidence:**
- Large geographic extent
- Long time span
- Diverse culture

**Support:** Many ancient civilizations were multilingual
**Confidence:** Possible but complicates analysis

## Comparison with Contemporary Scripts

### Proto-Elamite

**Similarities:**
- Undeciphered
- Mixed system suspected
- Administrative/economic use
- Geographic proximity

**Differences:**
- Different symbol forms
- Different development trajectory

**Conclusion:** Possible contact but systems are distinct

### Sumerian Cuneiform (Early)

**Similarities:**
- Mixed logographic-phonetic system
- Economic/administrative use
- Seal usage

**Differences:**
- Different media (clay vs. stone seals)
- Different forms
- Cuneiform evolved, Harappan disappeared

**Relevance:** Model of how mixed systems work

### Egyptian Hieroglyphics (Early)

**Similarities:**
- Mixed system
- Monumental and practical use
- Some pictographic elements

**Differences:**
- Much more extensively preserved
- Different linguistic substrate
- Writing direction and aesthetics differ

**Relevance:** Typological parallel

## Decipherment Challenges

### Why Harappan Remains Undeciphered

1. **Short texts** - Limited context
2. **No bilinguals** - No Rosetta Stone equivalent
3. **Unknown language** - Cannot use comparative method confidently
4. **Corpus preservation** - Limited, fragmented
5. **Lack of cross-references** - Internal inconsistency or external links
6. **Possible multiple text types** - Different genres/purposes confound analysis

### What Would Help Decipherment

- Longer texts (more context)
- Bilingual inscription (definitive breakthrough)
- Clear phonetic indicators
- More corpus (statistical power)
- Archaeological context (semantic clues)
