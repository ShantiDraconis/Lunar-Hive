## What Are Proto-Scripts?

**Definition:** Symbolic systems that precede full writing or represent transitional stages.

**Characteristics:**
- May lack full linguistic representation
- Often pictographic or symbolic
- Limited functional range (accounting, ownership, ritual)
- May not encode complete language

**Distinction from full writing:**
- Full writing can represent any utterance in the language
- Proto-writing may be limited to specific domains

## Proto-Cuneiform (Mesopotamia)

**Time period:** ~3400-3000 BCE
**Location:** Uruk and surrounding areas (modern Iraq)
**Status:** Partially deciphered (evolved into deciphered cuneiform)

### Characteristics

**Symbol count:** ~1200+ signs initially (reduced over time)
**Text types:** Economic, administrative
**Medium:** Clay tablets
**Direction:** Variable early, standardized later

### System Type

**Mixed logographic-numeric:**
- Logograms for commodities, places, titles
- Numeric signs with different bases for different goods
- Gradually incorporated phonetic elements

### Evolution to Cuneiform

- Pictographic â†’ abstract wedge forms
- Purely logographic â†’ logographic + phonetic
- Multiple sign values (polyvalency)
- Eventually represents Sumerian and Akkadian languages

### Relevance to Harappan

**Similarities:**
- Administrative/economic function
- Mixed system likely
- Seal usage

**Differences:**
- Cuneiform evolved and survived; Harappan did not
- Different symbol forms
- Clay vs. seal preference

**Confidence:** Useful typological model but distinct systems

## Proto-Elamite (Iran)

**Time period:** ~3200-2700 BCE
**Location:** Elam (southwestern Iran)
**Status:** Largely undeciphered (despite being contemporary with deciphered systems)

### Characteristics

**Symbol count:** ~1000+ signs
**Text types:** Administrative
**Medium:** Clay tablets
**Direction:** Variable

### Decipherment Challenges

- Unknown language substrate
- No clear bilingual texts
- Short texts with limited context
- Distinct from contemporary Sumerian

### Relevance to Harappan

**Similarities:**
- Undeciphered despite contextual information
- Administrative function
- Short texts
- Geographic proximity (relative to Mesopotamia)

**Lesson:** Even with good archaeological context, decipherment is not guaranteed.

**Confidence:** Cautionary parallel

## Early Egyptian (Proto-Hieroglyphic)

**Time period:** ~3300-3000 BCE (Predynastic to Early Dynastic)
**Location:** Egypt
**Status:** Partially understood (evolved into deciphered hieroglyphics)

### Characteristics

**Symbol count:** Few hundred signs early on
**Text types:** Labels, ownership marks, ritual/ceremonial
**Medium:** Pottery, stone, bone, ivory
**Direction:** Variable initially

### System Type

**Mixed from the start:**
- Logograms (word signs)
- Phonograms (sound signs)
- Determinatives (semantic classifiers)

### Evolution to Hieroglyphics

- Rapid standardization
- Three-way system solidified
- Used for monumental and administrative purposes

### Relevance to Harappan

**Similarities:**
- Early labels and ownership marks
- Pictographic elements
- Mixed system suspected

**Differences:**
- Egyptian script survived and evolved
- Extensive later corpus allows retrospective analysis
- Bilingual (Rosetta Stone) enabled decipherment

**Confidence:** Good typological model for mixed systems

## Cretan Scripts (Proto-Linear A/B)

**Time period:** ~2000-1450 BCE (earlier pictographic ~2100-1900 BCE)
**Location:** Crete
**Status:** Linear B deciphered (Greek), Linear A undeciphered

### Cretan Hieroglyphic

**Earlier system:** ~2100-1700 BCE
**Symbol count:** ~90 signs
**Status:** Undeciphered

### Linear A

**Time period:** ~1800-1450 BCE
**Symbol count:** ~90 signs
**Status:** Undeciphered (unknown language)

### Linear B

**Time period:** ~1450-1200 BCE
**Symbol count:** ~90 signs (simplified from Linear A)
**Status:** Deciphered 1952 (Michael Ventris) - represents Greek

**System type:** Syllabary + logograms
- CV and CVC syllables
- Logograms for common words
- Numeric systems

### Relevance to Harappan

**Similarities:**
- Syllabic system with logograms is possibility
- ~90 core signs similar to Harappan core set
- Administrative usage

**Differences:**
- Linear B deciphered via known language (Greek)
- Better preservation
- Different geographic/cultural context

**Lesson:** Syllabary + logogram mix works well, fits Harappan symbol count.

**Confidence:** Useful structural model

## Proto-Sinaitic / Proto-Canaanite

**Time period:** ~1900-1500 BCE
**Location:** Sinai Peninsula, Levant
**Status:** Partially understood (ancestor of alphabetic scripts)

### Characteristics

**Symbol count:** ~30 signs
**System type:** Abjad (consonant alphabet)
**Innovation:** Acrophonic principle (symbol represents first sound of pictured object)

### Evolution to Alphabet

- Proto-Sinaitic â†’ Phoenician â†’ Greek â†’ Latin (and others)
- Major innovation: small symbol set, purely phonetic
- Enabled widespread literacy

### Relevance to Harappan

**Difference:** Harappan has too many symbols for pure alphabet
**Lesson:** Alphabetic innovation is rare and revolutionary

**Confidence:** Harappan is NOT alphabetic

## Chinese Oracle Bone Script

**Time period:** ~1200 BCE (Shang Dynasty)
**Location:** China
**Status:** Deciphered (archaic Chinese)

### Characteristics

**Symbol count:** ~5000 signs (3000 identified)
**System type:** Logographic primarily, some phonetic elements
**Text types:** Divination records

### Relevance to Harappan

**Difference:** Much larger symbol inventory
**Similarity:** Mixed system, some phonetic use

**Lesson:** Logographic systems can persist and evolve without becoming phonetic

**Confidence:** Too different in scale to be close model

## Pre-Columbian American Scripts

### Olmec and Zapotec

**Time period:** ~900 BCE - 500 CE
**Location:** Mesoamerica
**Status:** Partially deciphered

**Relevance:** Independent invention of writing, different cultural context

### Maya

**Time period:** ~300 BCE - 1500 CE
**Status:** Substantially deciphered

**System type:** Logographic-syllabic mix
**Relevance:** Shows mixed systems can be very effective

### Rongorongo (Easter Island)

**Time period:** ~1200-1900 CE (uncertain)
**Status:** Undeciphered
**Relevance:** Small corpus, unknown language, may remain forever mysterious

**Lesson:** Not all scripts can or will be deciphered.

## Patterns Across Proto-Scripts

### Common Features

1. **Economic/administrative genesis** - Writing often emerges for record-keeping
2. **Pictographic origins** - Early forms are often pictorial
3. **Evolution toward abstraction** - Forms simplify over time
4. **Mixed systems common** - Logographic + phonetic is frequent pattern
5. **Seal usage** - Seals for ownership/identity widespread

### Divergent Paths

1. **Some evolved (Cuneiform, Hieroglyphic)** - Became full writing systems
2. **Some disappeared (Harappan, Proto-Elamite)** - Lost without descendants
3. **Some remained limited (Proto-Sinaitic)** - Didn't expand functionally before evolution
4. **Some transformed (Proto-Sinaitic â†’ Alphabet)** - Radical innovations

### Why Some Scripts Disappear

**Possible reasons:**
- Civilization collapse or transformation
- Displacement by more efficient system
- Language shift
- Change in social organization
- Loss of literacy class
- Material changes (different media)

**Harappan case:** Civilization declined ~1900-1700 BCE, script disappears with it.

## Lessons for Harappan Analysis

### From Deciphered Scripts

- Mixed systems are common and functional
- Context is crucial for interpretation
- Symbol count suggests syllabary or mixed system
- Bilinguals are game-changers (but Harappan lacks them)

### From Undeciphered Scripts

- Decipherment is not inevitable
- Short texts are major obstacle
- Unknown language substrate is nearly insurmountable
- Archaeological context helps but isn't sufficient

### Realistic Assessment

**Harappan may remain undeciphered unless:**
- Bilingual found
- Language affiliation proven
- Much longer texts discovered
- Radical methodological breakthrough
