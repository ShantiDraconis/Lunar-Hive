## Visual Form Comparisons

### Geometric Similarities

**Universal forms** (found in most scripts):
- Vertical/horizontal lines
- Circles and semicircles
- Cross patterns
- Triangular shapes

**Interpretation:** These are fundamental human drawing patterns, not evidence of connection.

### Distinctive Forms

**Harappan distinctive features:**
- "Fish" symbol and variants
- "Jar" symbol variants
- Specific compound patterns

**Comparison status:** Some superficial similarities to other scripts noted but no convincing systematic parallels.

**Confidence:** Low for historical connections.

## Functional Comparisons

### Administrative Usage

**Common pattern:**
- Harappan seals
- Cuneiform tablets
- Egyptian labels
- Cretan seals and tablets

**Interpretation:** Writing emerges for record-keeping cross-culturally.

**Relevance:** Functional convergence, not necessarily historical connection.

### Seal Technology

**Harappan vs. Mesopotamian seals:**
- Both used for identification
- Different aesthetics and iconography
- Possible trade contact (seals found across regions)

**Question:** Did seal technology transfer independently of script?

**Confidence:** Medium (trade contact documented, script influence unproven)

## Typological Patterns

### Mixed Systems

**Pattern:** Many ancient scripts mix logograms and phonetic elements.

**Scripts using this:**
- Sumerian/Akkadian cuneiform
- Egyptian hieroglyphics
- Maya glyphs
- Possibly Harappan

**Relevance:** If Harappan is mixed, it follows common typological pattern.

**Confidence:** Medium (typologically reasonable)

### Symbol Inventory Size

**~100-200 symbols:** Typically syllabaries
**~400-700 symbols:** Typically mixed logo-syllabic
**~1000+ symbols:** Logographic with extensive phonetic use
**~20-40 symbols:** Alphabetic/abjad

**Harappan (~400-700):** Fits mixed system profile best.
