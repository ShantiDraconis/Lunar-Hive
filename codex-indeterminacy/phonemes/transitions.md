# Phonetic Transitions

## Purpose

This document explores possible phonetic transitions and sound changes that may be observable in scripts, even when the phonetic values themselves are uncertain.

## Status

üîÑ **Speculative** - Based on typological patterns, not proven Harappan data.

## What Are Phonetic Transitions?

Phonetic transitions are:
- Sound changes over time (diachronic)
- Sound changes in context (synchronic, allophonic)
- Sound changes due to borrowing (contact linguistics)
- Articulatory adjustments between sounds

## Why Study Transitions?

Even without knowing exact phonetic values, transitions may be detectable through:
- Spelling variations
- Symbol substitutions
- Regional differences
- Temporal changes

## Types of Phonetic Transitions

### 1. Assimilation

**Definition:** Sounds become more similar to neighboring sounds.

**Common patterns:**
- Nasal assimilation: /n/ ‚Üí /m/ before /p, b/ (impossible to pronounce /np/, natural as /mp/)
- Voicing assimilation: /s/ ‚Üí /z/ between vowels
- Place assimilation: /t/ ‚Üí /k/ before /k/

**Observable in writing:**
- Spelling variations (if script is phonetic)
- Alternate forms of same word
- Regional variants

**Harappan evidence:** Insufficient data to confirm.
**Confidence:** Hypothetical

### 2. Dissimilation

**Definition:** Sounds become less similar to avoid repetition.

**Common patterns:**
- /r...r/ ‚Üí /l...r/ (Latin: peregrinus ‚Üí pellegrino)
- Aspiration dissimilation (Sanskrit: bharati but not *bharathi)

**Harappan evidence:** None yet.
**Confidence:** Speculative

### 3. Lenition (Weakening)

**Definition:** Sounds become less constricted, more vowel-like.

**Common patterns:**
- /p, t, k/ ‚Üí /b, d, g/ (voicing)
- /b, d, g/ ‚Üí /Œ≤, √∞, …£/ (fricativization)
- /t/ ‚Üí /…æ/ (flapping, as in American English "water")
- Consonant loss intervocalically

**Observable in writing:**
- Later forms show loss of consonants
- Spelling becomes more ambiguous
- Silent letters emerge

**Harappan evidence:** Requires temporal stratification.
**Confidence:** Unknown (no clear diachronic data)

### 4. Fortition (Strengthening)

**Definition:** Sounds become more constricted or emphasized.

**Common patterns:**
- /w, j/ ‚Üí /gw, gj/ (glide strengthening)
- Voiceless to aspirated stops
- Glottal stop insertion (Arabic, German)

**Harappan evidence:** None.
**Confidence:** Speculative

### 5. Metathesis

**Definition:** Sounds switch positions.

**Common patterns:**
- /ask/ ‚Üí /aks/ (English: ask ‚Üí ax)
- Liquid metathesis common (/r/ and /l/)

**Observable in writing:**
- Alternate spellings with reversed consonants

**Harappan evidence:** Would require variant inscriptions.
**Confidence:** Unknown

### Common Historical Patterns

**Grimm's Law (Indo-European):**
- Systematic consonant shift: /p/ ‚Üí /f/, /t/ ‚Üí /Œ∏/, /k/ ‚Üí /h/
- Example: Latin pater ‚Üí English father

**Great Vowel Shift (English):**
- Long vowels raised or diphthongized
- Example: /iÀê/ ‚Üí /a…™/ (Middle English "tyme" ‚Üí Modern "time")

**Dravidian Sound Changes:**
- Weakening of word-initial voicing
- Gemination (consonant doubling)
- Retroflexion of dental consonants

**Relevance:** If language family is identified, expected sound changes can be tested.

## Synchronic Transitions (Allophonic Variation)

### Context-Dependent Pronunciation

**Allophones:** Different pronunciations of same phoneme.

**Examples:**
- /p/ aspirated word-initially [p ∞], unaspirated after /s/ [p]
- /t/ dental before dental, alveolar elsewhere
- /n/ velar before /k, g/ [≈ã]

**Relevance to writing:**
- If script is precise, may mark allophones
- If script is phonemic, allophones not distinguished
- Cannot determine without phonetic knowledge

### Sandhi (Sanskrit Term for Sound Junction)

**Definition:** Changes at word or morpheme boundaries.

**Common patterns:**
- Vowel coalescence: /a + i/ ‚Üí /e/
- Consonant assimilation at boundaries
- Deletion or insertion of sounds

**Observable in writing:**
- Compound words spelled differently than components
- Boundary markers or their absence

**Harappan evidence:** Would require word boundary identification.
**Confidence:** Unknown

## Articulatory Transitions

### Natural Transition Sequences

**Easy transitions (articulatorily simple):**
- CV (consonant-vowel): universal
- Nasal + stop with same place: /mp/, /nt/, /≈ãk/
- Liquid + consonant: /rt/, /lt/

**Difficult transitions (articulatorily complex):**
- Stop + stop with different places: /pt/, /kg/ (less common)
- Complex clusters: /kstr/, /pfl/

**Implication:** Symbol sequences may reflect articulatory ease.

### Phonotactic Constraints

**Universal tendencies:**
- Sonority sequencing: Syllables rise in sonority to peak (vowel), then fall
- Example: /stra/ is natural (s < t < r < a), /atrs/ is unnatural

**Language-specific:**
- Some languages allow /tl/ (Nahuatl), others don't (English)
- Some allow word-initial /≈ã/ (Viet), others don't (English)

**Harappan:** Unknown constraints without phonetic knowledge.

## Comparative Transition Patterns

### Dravidian

**Characteristic changes:**
- Weakening of initial voicing
- Gemination (doubling) of medial consonants
- Retroflexion influenced by /r/
- Vowel harmony in some subgroups

**If Harappan is Dravidian:** Look for these patterns.

### Indo-Aryan

**Characteristic changes:**
- Loss of final consonants in modern languages
- Aspiration contrasts maintained or lost
- Cluster simplification
- Vowel reduction in unstressed syllables

**If Harappan has Indo-Aryan influence:** Look for these patterns.

### Austro-Asiatic (Munda)

**Characteristic changes:**
- Loss of word-final consonants
- Sesquisyllabic structure (minor syllable + major syllable)
- Vowel quality changes

**If Harappan is Munda:** Look for these patterns.

## Statistical Approaches to Transitions

### Symbol Substitution Analysis

**Method:**
- Identify inscriptions that are nearly identical
- Note which symbols differ
- Hypothesize these are phonetically similar

**Example:**
- If inscription A has symbol X and inscription B has symbol Y in same position
- Hypothesis: X and Y may represent similar sounds

**Confidence:** Requires many parallel inscriptions (rare in Harappan).

### Positional Variation

**Method:**
- Track symbol distribution by position
- Note if certain symbols never precede/follow others
- Infer phonetic incompatibility

**Example:**
- If symbol A never follows symbol B
- Hypothesis: Phonetic sequence AB is prohibited (phonotactic constraint)

**Confidence:** Requires large corpus and statistical validation.

## Falsification Criteria

### Would Support Transition Hypothesis:
- Consistent spelling variations across inscriptions
- Temporal stratification shows systematic changes
- Regional variations show geographic patterns
- Parallels with known sound changes in related languages

### Would Weaken Hypothesis:
- Random variation with no pattern
- Alternative explanation (scribal error, not phonetic change)
- Insufficient data to confirm pattern

### Would Falsify:
- Decipherment shows no phonetic relationship between variant symbols
- Archaeological evidence contradicts temporal or geographic assumptions
