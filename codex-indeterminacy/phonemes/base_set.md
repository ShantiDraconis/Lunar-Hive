# Base Phoneme Set

## Purpose

This document explores possible phonetic inventories for undeciphered scripts based on comparative linguistics, typological universals, and articulatory constraints.

## Epistemic Warning

**We cannot know the actual sounds of undeciphered languages.**

This document presents:
- Typologically likely phoneme inventories
- Comparisons with related language families
- Articulatory possibilities
- Statistical expectations

**This is NOT:**
- A proven phonetic system
- A definitive phonology
- A translation key

## Methodology

### Comparative Approach
1. Survey related language families (if known/hypothesized)
2. Identify common phonemes across these families
3. Note typological universals
4. Propose probable phoneme inventory

### Typological Approach
1. Survey world's languages for phoneme frequency
2. Identify universal or near-universal sounds
3. Note rare or unusual sounds
4. Propose typologically unmarked inventory

### Articulatory Approach
1. Identify easiest/most natural articulations
2. Note universal articulatory constraints
3. Consider acquisition order (child language)
4. Propose articulatorily simple inventory

## Harappan/Indus Valley: Phonetic Possibilities

### Language Affiliation Hypotheses

**Hypothesis 1: Dravidian**
- Geographic proximity to Dravidian languages
- Some scholars propose Dravidian connection
- Confidence: Low (no proof, much debate)

**Hypothesis 2: Indo-Aryan (Early Vedic)**
- Later dominance of Indo-Aryan in region
- But may postdate Harappan
- Confidence: Very low (temporal issues)

**Hypothesis 3: Austro-Asiatic (Munda)**
- Present in region, may be ancient
- Limited comparative work
- Confidence: Very low (speculative)

**Hypothesis 4: Language Isolate**
- May not belong to known family
- Cannot use comparative method
- Confidence: Unknown (absence of evidence)

**Hypothesis 5: Multiple Languages**
- Harappan civilization was large and diverse
- May have multilingual script usage
- Confidence: Possible but complicates analysis

## Typological Universals

### Most Common Phonemes (Cross-Linguistic)

**Consonants appearing in >90% of world's languages:**
- /m, p, b, t, d, k, g, n, s, w, j/

**Vowels appearing in >90% of languages:**
- /a, i, u/

**Implication:** Any phoneme inventory likely includes these.

### Rare Phonemes

**Uncommon in world's languages:**
- Click consonants (Khoisan, some Bantu)
- Pharyngeal consonants (Semitic)
- Tone (common in Asia, Africa; less common in Indo-European)
- Nasal vowels (Romance, African; not universal)

**Implication:** Unless evidence suggests otherwise, assume unmarked inventory.

## Symbol-to-Phoneme Mapping Constraints

### Inventory Size Constraint

**Harappan symbol count:** ~400-700 signs (estimates vary)
**Phoneme inventory:** Typically 20-40 phonemes

**Implication:** Script is NOT purely phonetic (like alphabet).

**Possibilities:**
1. Logographic-phonetic mix (like Egyptian, Sumerian)
2. Syllabary (symbols represent CV combinations)
3. Abjad (consonants only, vowels implied)
4. Abugida (consonants with vowel diacritics)
5. Pure logographic (no phonetic component)

**Confidence:** Cannot determine from symbol count alone.

## Phonotactic Constraints

### Universal Constraints
- All languages have CV (consonant-vowel) syllables
- Most languages have V (vowel-only) syllables
- Many languages have CVC syllables
- Complex clusters (CCCV) are rarer

**Implication:** Likely syllable structures include CV, V, CVC.

### Language-Family Specific

**Dravidian typical:**
- Prefers open syllables (CV)
- Avoids initial clusters
- Allows final nasals
- Geminate consonants

**Indo-Aryan typical:**
- Allows initial clusters
- Allows complex syllables
- Sanskrit: very permissive phonotactics

**Cannot determine without more evidence.**

## Phonetic Processes

### Common Sound Changes
- Assimilation (sounds becoming more similar)
- Dissimilation (sounds becoming less similar)
- Lenition (weakening of articulation)
- Fortition (strengthening of articulation)
- Metathesis (reordering of sounds)

**Relevance:** If diachronic analysis possible, these processes guide reconstruction.

### Prosodic Features

**Tone:**
- Presence of tone doubles functional phonemes
- Tone languages: /ma/ with high vs. low tone = different words
- Evidence for tone: typically requires native speaker testimony
- Harappan: No way to determine if tonal

**Stress:**
- Placement of emphasis on syllables
- Can be lexical (word-specific) or predictable
- Usually not written in ancient scripts
- Harappan: Unknown

**Length:**
- Vowel or consonant duration distinctions
- Common in Dravidian (vowel length)
- Common in Sanskrit (vowel and consonant length)
- May or may not be marked in writing

## Comparative Phonological Patterns

### Proto-Dravidian Reconstruction
- Reconstructed by linguists
- ~25-30 consonants, ~10 vowels
- Retroflex consonants characteristic
- Verb-final word order (not phonological but syntactic)

**If Harappan is Dravidian:** This inventory is hypothesis base.

### Proto-Indo-European
- Well-reconstructed
- Complex consonant system
- Ablaut (vowel alternation patterns)
- Laryngeals (lost in daughter languages)

**If Harappan has Indo-European contact:** May see borrowings.

## Falsification Criteria

### Would Support Specific Phonology:
- Bilingual inscription discovered
- Clear phonetic indicators in script
- Convincing cognates with known language
- Archaeological linguistic evidence

### Would Weaken Hypothesis:
- Typologically implausible inventory
- Violates articulatory constraints
- Doesn't match symbol inventory size
- Better alternative model emerges

### Would Falsify:
- Definitive decipherment shows different system
- Language affiliation proven different
- Phonetic analysis contradicts articulatory possibility
